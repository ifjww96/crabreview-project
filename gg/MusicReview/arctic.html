Over the last eight years, the Arctic Monkeys have gone from spastic punk, to doomed stoner rock, to sparkling guitar pop, to their fifth album’s skinny-jeaned funk. On AM, the quartet, now based in Los Angeles, offer a paranoid, haunted collection that goes beyond the sweaty clubs and furtive flirts into the hotel rooms, after parties, and bad decisions that can follow.


When Arctic Monkeys released AM in 2013, it felt like a midnight phone call you weren’t expecting—intimate, disoriented, and impossible to ignore. This wasn’t the scrappy Sheffield band that had taken the world by storm with jittery tales of dancefloors and youthful bravado. AM was slower, sexier, darker. It was a reinvention in both sound and soul—a record that traded indie-rock urgency for something far more brooding and magnetic. From the very first notes of “Do I Wanna Know?”, the tone is set: a sludgy, hypnotic guitar riff spirals slowly while Alex Turner’s voice slinks in with a mix of vulnerability and swagger. It’s a song about the weight of unspoken feelings, about wanting someone but not knowing if you should—or if they even care. That emotional tug-of-war defines the entire album. The riffs are heavy and minimal, the beats are precise and deliberate, and Turner's lyrics are sharp enough to cut through even the thickest haze of late-night longing. Across the album, Arctic Monkeys dip into a rich palette of sounds: hip-hop-inspired grooves, R&B smoothness, and classic rock influences from T. Rex to The Rolling Stones. Yet somehow, it never feels like imitation. Instead, it feels like evolution. There’s a smoky sophistication to AM that had only been hinted at in earlier records. Where their 2009 album Humbug (produced by Josh Homme) was their first flirtation with desert rock darkness, AM fully commits to the mood—and somehow makes it sexy. “No. 1 Party Anthem” and “Mad Sounds” stand out as the album’s emotional core, drifting away from the snarling riffs and into softer, more melancholic territory. “No. 1 Party Anthem” in particular plays like a hazy memory of a night that didn’t quite go the way you planned—a quiet lament for something lost before it was even found. Turner’s lyrics feel both cinematic and brutally personal, conjuring images of smoke-filled rooms, regretful glances, and the fleeting comfort of temporary love. Then there’s “R U Mine?”, a blistering track that captures the heart of the album’s restless energy. “Are you mine tomorrow, or just mine tonight?” Turner asks, distilling an entire generation’s anxieties about love and connection into one simple question. It's a line that feels as relevant now as it did then—especially in an era where relationships can begin and end in the span of a swipe. The final track, “I Wanna Be Yours,” brings the album full circle—not with a bang, but a whisper. Borrowing the words of punk poet John Cooper Clarke, Turner transforms domestic metaphors into declarations of devotion. “I wanna be your vacuum cleaner, breathing in your dust,” he croons, in a tone that makes the ridiculous sound romantic. It’s a fitting end to an album that’s constantly navigating the line between sincerity and performance, passion and emptiness. What makes AM such a compelling record is its sense of mood. It’s not an album you just listen to—it’s one you inhabit. It wraps around you like a leather jacket still warm from someone else’s body. It doesn’t scream for attention, it draws you in with a smirk and a raised eyebrow. Turner is no longer the fast-talking observer of British nightlife; he’s become the lonesome poet, the late-night driver, the man making questionable decisions under fluorescent hotel lighting. In many ways, AM is Arctic Monkeys at their most confident—and their most confused. It’s a contradiction wrapped in velvet: glossy but gritty, emotional yet aloof, cool but undeniably earnest. And that tension is exactly what makes it one of the defining rock albums of the 2010s. It’s not just a record about love, lust, and late nights—it’s a record about the spaces in between. The texts that go unanswered. The eyes that meet across crowded rooms. The longing that stays long after the lights come up.
